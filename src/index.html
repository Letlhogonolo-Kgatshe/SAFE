        Financial Definitions

        Learn essential financial terms with simple explanations, organized by category.

        Budgeting & Planning

        Budgeting

        Budgeting is the process of creating a plan to manage your income and expenses. For example, using a 50/30/20 rule, you allocate 50% of your income to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings or debt repayment.

        Example: Budgeting for monthly groceries, transport, entertainment, and savings.

        Financial Plans

        A financial plan combines your income, expenses, savings, and investments to achieve medium- and long-term financial goals. It helps you stay on track with your budget and prepares you for future events like education, retirement, or buying a home.

        Example: A family saving for university contributes R2,000 monthly to a Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA), leveraging tax-free growth for 15 years.

        Emergency Fund

        An emergency fund is money set aside to cover unexpected expenses like medical bills, car repairs, or job loss. It usually covers 3‚Äì6 months of living costs.

        Example: Keeping R50,000 aside for medical emergencies or job loss.

        Income & Expenses

        Saving

        Saving involves setting aside money for future use, like an emergency fund or a big purchase. For instance, saving R500 monthly in a high-interest account can grow over time.

        Assets vs. Liabilities

        Assets are things that generate income or appreciate in value, like property, stocks, or businesses.

        Liabilities are things that cost you money, like debt, loans, and expenses.

        Example: Owning a home (asset) that increases in value vs. paying off a car loan (liability) that reduces your financial position.

        Income vs. Expenditure

        Income is the money you earn, whether from a salary, business, or investments.

        Expenditure is the money you spend on living costs, such as food, rent, utilities, and debt repayments.

        Example: A monthly salary vs. monthly rent payments. Reducing unnecessary expenditure helps increase your savings.

        Cash Flow

        The movement of money in and out of your account. Positive cash flow occurs when income exceeds expenses, while negative cash flow means you're spending more than you earn.

        Example: You earn R10,000 per month, but your total expenses are R8,000. You have R2,000 in positive cash flow to save or invest.

        Investing

        Types of Investments

        Different investments (stocks, bonds, real estate) offer varying returns and risk profiles.

        Example: Use EasyEquities to buy stocks and ETFs for growth, bond ETFs for stability, and consider real estate for income and value appreciation.

        Diversification

        Diversification means spreading investments across various asset classes to minimize risk. By diversifying, you can protect yourself from market fluctuations in one sector.

        Example: Rather than investing all your money in one stock, you could invest in a mix of stocks, bonds, real estate, and a retirement plan to balance risk.

        Asset Allocation & Diversification

        Asset allocation involves dividing your portfolio among different asset classes, while diversification spreads investments within those classes to reduce risk.

        Example: Create a diversified portfolio on EasyEquities by investing in local and international ETFs and stocks from different sectors.

        Compound Interest

        Compound interest is the interest on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods, accelerating growth over time.

        Example: R10,000 earning 8% annually grows to R14,693 after five years of compounding.

        Markets & Products

        ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)

        An ETF is a collection of stocks or bonds that tracks an index and is traded on a stock exchange. It provides diversification and usually has low fees.

        Example: Investing in a SATRIX 40 ETF to gain exposure to the JSE's top-performing companies.

        Unit Trust

        A unit trust is a fund where many investors pool their money, which is then managed by professionals to invest in stocks, bonds, or property.

        Example: Saving R5,000 monthly in a balanced unit trust to fund future purchases.

        JSE (Johannesburg Stock Exchange)

        The JSE is South Africa‚Äôs main stock market where shares of companies are bought and sold. It allows investors to buy ownership in businesses.

        Example: Buying MTN shares at R130 each through an online stockbroker.

        JSE Hours & Days

        The JSE operates Monday to Friday, from 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM, excluding South African public holidays.

        Example: Trading Sasol shares on a Tuesday afternoon during market hours.

        Alternative Stocks

        Alternative stocks refer to investments outside traditional sectors, such as renewable energy, commodities, or emerging tech companies.

        Example: Investing in a green energy firm listed on the JSE.

        Property

        Property investment involves buying real estate to earn rental income or benefit from long-term value increases.

        Example: Owning a R2 million apartment that generates R15,000 monthly in rental income.

        Stocks

        Stocks are shares of ownership in a company. Investors can earn money through price increases (capital gains) and dividends.

        Example: Investing in Woolworths shares for steady dividend payouts.

        Dividends

        Dividends are payments made by a company to its shareholders from its profits. They provide regular income to investors.

        Example: Receiving R2,000 in annual dividends for holding 1,000 shares.

        Bull Market

        A bull market occurs when stock prices are rising over time, indicating strong investor confidence and economic growth.

        Example: The JSE Top 40 index gains 20% over six months due to strong corporate earnings.

        Bear Market

        A bear market happens when stock prices drop 20% or more, often caused by economic slowdowns or negative investor sentiment.

        Example: The JSE falls sharply during a recession, leading to a bear market.

        S&P 500

        The S&P 500 is a U.S. stock market index that tracks the 500 largest companies. It is used to measure the overall performance of the American stock market.

        Example: Investing in an ETF tracking the S&P 500 for international diversification.

        Top 40

        The Top 40 refers to the 40 largest companies listed on the JSE, by market value. It provides a snapshot of South Africa‚Äôs major businesses.

        Example: Investing in a Top 40 ETF for exposure to leading South African firms.

        Trust Fund

        A trust fund is a legal structure that holds money or assets on behalf of someone, usually for long-term care or inheritance purposes.

        Example: Parents setting up a trust fund for their children‚Äôs education.

        Middle Class

        The middle class includes people or families who earn enough to afford basic needs and some extra comforts. They usually have steady jobs and savings.

        Example: A family earning R20,000 monthly, affording housing, schooling, and leisure activities.

        Debt & Credit

        Credit Score

        Your credit score is a number that represents how reliable you are at repaying borrowed money. Lenders use it to decide whether to approve your loan or credit application.

        Example: A score above 650 increases the likelihood of securing a home loan with better terms.

        Credit Card

        A credit card allows you to borrow money up to a certain limit to make purchases. If you pay off the balance each month, you avoid interest and build a good credit history.

        Example: Spending R5,000 monthly on a credit card and paying it off to avoid 18% annual interest charges.

        Balloon Payment

        A balloon payment is a large, final payment due at the end of a loan term. It allows for smaller monthly payments during the loan period, but requires paying a big amount at the end.

        Example: Paying R90,000 as a balloon payment for a R300,000 car after five years of financing.

        Qualify vs. Afford

        Qualifying means a lender agrees to lend you a certain amount based on your credit profile. Affording means whether the loan repayments comfortably fit within your monthly budget.

        Example: Qualifying for a R1 million loan but only affording repayments on R700,000 due to personal expenses.

        Buying First Home

        Buying your first home involves saving for a deposit, getting a home loan (bond), and understanding additional costs like transfer duties and legal fees.

        Example: Buying a R1 million home with a 10% deposit (R100,000) and covering R25,000 in bond registration fees.

        Buying First Car

        Buying your first car means choosing between new or used vehicles, saving for a deposit, and deciding whether to pay cash or use vehicle financing.

        Example: Buying a R150,000 used car with a 10% deposit (R15,000) and financing over three years.

        Financing

        Financing means borrowing money to buy something now and paying it back over time with interest. It‚Äôs common for large purchases like cars or homes.

        Example: Financing a car with a R200,000 loan at 11% interest for five years.

        Interest Rate

        The interest rate is the cost of borrowing money or the return earned on savings. It's shown as a percentage and can significantly affect loan repayments or investment returns.

        Example: Borrowing R100,000 at a 10% interest rate costs R10,000 annually in interest.

        Prime Rate

        The prime rate is the base interest rate banks use when lending to customers. It is influenced by the central bank and affects the cost of loans.

        Example: If the prime rate is 11%, a home loan may be offered at 12%, reflecting the borrower's risk profile.

        Savings & Retirement

        Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA)

        A TFSA is a savings or investment account where any interest, dividends, or capital gains earned are completely tax-free. There are annual and lifetime contribution limits, making it ideal for long-term growth.

        Example: Investing in a TFSA linked to the JSE Top 40 index, earning tax-free capital gains over a decade.

        Retirement

        Retirement planning involves setting aside money to cover your living expenses after you stop working. This includes estimating future needs, accounting for inflation, and investing in retirement products like pension funds or retirement annuities (RAs).

        Example: Saving 15% of your income in an RA that grows at 8% annually to amass R2.5 million by age 65.

        Retirement Savings

        Contributing to a retirement savings plan (such as a pension fund or retirement annuity) ensures long-term wealth and security.

        Example: The South African government offers tax incentives for contributing to retirement savings plans.

        Inflation & Its Impact

        Inflation reduces the purchasing power of money over time, affecting the real value of your savings and investments.

        Example: Invest in stocks, REITs, or inflation-protected securities like TIPS through EasyEquities to stay ahead of inflation.

        Tax Efficiency

        Tax efficiency is about minimizing tax liabilities on your investments to maximize returns.

        Example: Use a Tax Free Savings Account or Retirement Annuity on EasyEquities to grow wealth while reducing tax exposure.

        Risk & Technology

        Risk & Risk Tolerance

        Risk tolerance is the level of variability in investment returns you're comfortable with.

        Example: If you're risk-averse, invest in bonds; if you‚Äôre risk-tolerant, consider growth stocks or crypto via EasyEquities, but always diversify.

        Financial Literacy

        Financial literacy is the understanding of key financial concepts like budgeting, investing, and credit.

        Example: Improve your financial literacy through Coursera or LinkedIn Learning, and track your credit with ClearScore.

        Financial Advisors & Technology

        Financial advisors provide expert guidance, while technology platforms simplify managing finances.

        Example: Use EasyEquities, ClearScore, and TradingView to manage your investments, and consult a financial advisor for tailored advice.

        Banking

        Banking

        Understanding different South African banks and account types can help you manage your money effectively. Compare options like savings accounts, current accounts, and investment-linked accounts.

        Example: Using Capitec for low-fee banking or FNB for integrated investment options.

        Personal Finance Goals

        Personal Finance Goals

        Setting clear financial goals‚Äîwhether saving for a car, wedding, or retirement‚Äîhelps you prioritize and plan your finances.

        Example: Saving R10,000 over 6 months for a wedding by cutting unnecessary expenses.
